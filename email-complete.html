Navigating the USCIS I-693 Immigration Medical Exam and Related Waivers

For U.S. immigration applicants, Form I-693, completed by a USCIS-designated Civil Surgeon, is an essential part of many green card applications. The exam focuses on communicable diseases of public health significance, required immunizations, and medical conditions that may make an applicant inadmissible under U.S. immigration law.

This is not a general annual physical and does not include routine preventive screening such as cholesterol or kidney function tests. Applicants should arrange a separate regular check-up, which is often covered by insurance, while the immigration medical exam usually is not.

âœ“ The exam typically includes a medical history, physical examination, tuberculin testing (or chest x-ray if needed), blood tests for syphilis and gonorrhea (where applicable), and a review of vaccination status and admissibility.
âœ“ Applicants must bring a printed, single-sided Form I-693, vaccination records if available, valid photo ID, and payment for the exam and form completion.
âœ“ A follow-up visit is usually required to review lab results, complete vaccinations, and have the Civil Surgeon sign and seal the I-693 envelope, which must remain unopened for USCIS.
âœ“ The latest I-693 edition (version dated 01/20/2025, effective 7/3/2025) must be used to avoid rejection.

Vaccination rules follow CDC guidance and are tailored to age, including vaccines such as Tdap, MMR, Varicella, Influenza, and Hepatitis B. If records are missing, applicants may undergo immunity testing or receive vaccines based on medical advice.

âœ“ Medical waivers for vaccines (for example, during pregnancy or where contraindications exist) can be granted directly by the Civil Surgeon.
âœ“ Religious or moral waivers require filing Form I-601, Application for Waiver of Grounds of Inadmissibility, directly with USCIS.
âœ“ Waivers involve extra fees, and applicants should bring a detailed health history for proper evaluation.

Certain applicants who are otherwise inadmissible due to specific medical grounds, vaccine refusals based on religion or morals, or other listed inadmissibility reasons may apply for a Form I-601 waiver. This application usually includes evidence of extreme hardship to qualifying U.S. relatives, and legal advice is strongly recommended.

âœ“ As of 10 April 2024, a properly completed and sealed Form I-693 signed on or after 1 November 2023 has indefinite validity.
âœ“ Applicants should normally collect their sealed I-693 from the clinic within 30 days of exam completion and submit it to USCIS within 60 days of the Civil Surgeon's signature to prevent delays or repeat testing.

Expert civil surgeons, such as Dr. Neema Malhotra, can coordinate vaccination coverage with insurance, arrange discounted lab fees, and offer expedited, concierge-style support for lab orders, waiver consultations, and copies of completed forms.

For detailed FAQs and official instructions, consult USCIS Clinic FAQs and the official USCIS waiver page at USCIS Form I-601.

Navigating Work Visa Applications for Canada and Short-Term Business Travel to Germany

Professionals planning cross-border work or business trips must understand the different requirements for Canadian work permits and short-stay Schengen visas for Germany. Careful preparation can prevent delays, refusals, and unexpected travel disruptions.

Most foreign nationals who want to work in Canada require a work permit issued by IRCC.

âœ“ In many cases, applicants first need a valid job offer from a Canadian employer and, where required, a positive Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) to show there is no negative impact on Canadian workers.
âœ“ Applications typically include a passport, proof of work experience, education and, where needed, an Educational Credential Assessment, language test results, police certificates, and proof of funds.
âœ“ Work permits can be employer-specific, open, or LMIA-exempt under certain agreements and programs.
âœ“ Processing often ranges from 2 to 6 months, and applicants may need biometrics and a medical exam depending on the job type and country of residence.

For detailed Canadian work permit information, see Globalization Partners.

For short-term business travel to Germany (up to 90 days in any 180-day period), many travellers require a Schengen visa.

âœ“ Permanent residents of Canada and those with valid Canadian study or work permits can apply at the German Consulate in Canada; others must apply from their country of residence.
âœ“ Applications may be lodged up to six months before travel, and processing usually takes at least 15 days.
âœ“ Appointments are mandatory and are booked online; missing documents can lead to refusals.
âœ“ Applicants must submit fingerprints and attend in person; even with a visa, final entry is decided by border officers based on security and admissibility considerations.

Best practices for both Canada and Germany include applying as early as possible, keeping copies of all supporting documents, following official document checklists carefully, and coordinating closely with employers or hosts to ensure accuracy.

Navigating the New Brunswick Experience Stream for Permanent Residency

The New Brunswick Experience stream under the New Brunswick Provincial Nominee Program (NBPNP) offers a targeted pathway to permanent residence for skilled workers already living and working in the province. It is designed for candidates who have proven their ability to integrate into New Brunswick's labour market and communities.

Key eligibility criteria focus on work, residence, language, education, and employer status.

âœ“ Applicants must hold full-time (minimum 30 hours per week), non-seasonal employment with an eligible New Brunswick employer and have at least six months of continuous work experience with that employer.
âœ“ Candidates must have lived continuously in New Brunswick for at least six months before applying.
âœ“ Language proficiency must meet at least Canadian Language Benchmark (CLB) 4 in reading, writing, listening, and speaking, proven by an approved language test such as IELTS General Training or CELPIP.
âœ“ Educational credentials must match the job's National Occupational Classification requirements, and foreign education typically requires a valid Educational Credential Assessment completed within the last five years.
âœ“ Employers must be actively operating in New Brunswick, comply with labour standards, show genuine labour shortages through recruitment efforts, and be in good legal standing.

The application process is structured in several stages.

âœ“ Secure a qualifying job offer from an eligible employer.
âœ“ Submit an Expression of Interest (EOI) through the INB online portal.
âœ“ Upon receiving an Invitation to Apply (ITA), submit a full application with supporting documents within 45 days.
âœ“ If approved, obtain a provincial nomination certificate, valid for six months, and then apply to IRCC for permanent residence.
âœ“ After IRCC's medical, security, and criminality checks and final approval, confirm permanent resident landing and report it via the INB portal within 30 days.

Applicants must provide complete documentation, including language test results, proof of employment and residence, educational certificates, and certified translations for any documents not in English or French. Both the applicant and employer must ensure accuracy and honesty to reduce the risk of refusal.

Benefits of this stream include a direct route to permanent residence with strong provincial backing, options to include eligible family members, and the opportunity to settle long term in a province known for its welcoming communities and stable labour market. Candidates should maintain valid temporary status in Canada throughout the process and may wish to consult regulated immigration professionals for up-to-date advice.
